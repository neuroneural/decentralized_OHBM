Data Is the New Oil (Again)

Large, diverse cohorts are the foremost need in neuroimaging
  • Statistical power for tiny effects
    Brain–behavior $r$ is small; reproducible BWAS need $N$ in the thousands.
  • Generalization beyond one scanner
    Multi-site heterogeneity acts as a regularizer toward protocol-invariant signal.
  • Representativeness & equity
    Cohorts are WEIRD- and ancestry-skewed; diversity is both a validity and a fairness argument.
  • Rare conditions & subtypes.
    No single center has the cases; multi-site is the only way to build the cohort.
WEIRD = Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich & Democratic [Henrich et al., 2010] · BWAS power [Marek et al., 2022]

…and Foundation Models Are Hungry

yet, globally, we have amassed
>1 billion MRI scans
Sources: [global MRI volumes (~100–150M/yr)] · [imaging share of healthcare data]
This is the age of foundation models — everywhere, and now in neuroimaging. But look at the scale gap: frontier LLMs train on tens of trillions of tokens; the best brain-imaging foundation models train on ~50-100k scans (BrainIAC ~49k, BrainLM ~77k, MR-RATE ~98k). Meanwhile the world acquires ~100-150M MRI scans every year (~40M in the US alone); brain & spine are >50% of studies, so tens of millions of brain MRIs annually. Medical imaging is ~70% of all stored healthcare data, yet <10% of images are ever retrieved after 60 days. So the addressable pool is hundreds of millions of brain scans sitting dark in PACS and research archives — and our FMs touch well under 0.1% of even a single year. That locked "dark knowledge" is exactly what federation could unlock. (Numbers are order-of-magnitude, off-the-cuff: collectiveminds.health MRI volumes; Scality/ industry PACS figures.)

Automation
the pipeline runs itself

Everything ENIGMA, plus…

  • Enables iterative algorithms
  • Simplifies single-shot methods
  • Dramatically faster — no waiting on coordination & DUAs
Three ways to mine multi-site data. OpenNeuro = fully shared, centralized: easiest to work with, full access to anonymized data, and it buys all four "data is the new oil" advantages — but only for the slice of the world that can legally be shared openly. ENIGMA = data stays local, meta-analysis: gets everything OpenNeuro does, PLUS (1) privacy/regulation/governance — structural MRI is identifiable (faces reconstruct from T1s even after defacing), it's PHI under HIPAA/GDPR/DUAs/IRB, so many sites legally cannot release raw data; FL shares only model updates, respects data-localization / sovereignty, and avoids a single centralized honeypot; (2) logistics/continual data — moving petabyte-scale imaging is slow and costly, compute where the data lives, and sites keep acquiring (continual contribution); (3) incentives/trust — sites that keep control and credit participate; federation lowers the political barrier, which is usually the real bottleneck, not the math. Downsides: no direct access to the data (the privacy is the point), and it's mostly manual meta-analysis. Automation is the third axis: it makes federation iterative (not just one-shot), simplifies single-shot methods, and — crucially — is dramatically faster, not just in compute but by removing the long wait for human coordination and DUAs. This sets up COINSTAC / NeuroFLAME.

Two orthogonal axes that cut across all three categories (methods, frameworks, platforms). LEFT - Analysis vs Learning (methods vs models): analysis shares statistics/sufficient stats (VBM, GLM single-shot; ICA, dFNC iterative); learning trains a shared model and is almost always multi-shot (FedAvg, FedSGD, deep nets). Both can be light or communication- heavy - the single-shot/multi-shot axis is a spectrum, explained verbally. RIGHT - Privacy: plausibly private by default (only derivatives leave a site, no formal guarantee); differential privacy adds calibrated noise but forces an epsilon budget across every category; HE/SMPC give cryptographic guarantees at heavy compute/comms cost. COINSTAC/NeuroFLAME support both workloads and multiple privacy levels.

Challenges of Federating Models

Params: [GPT-3 175B] · [BiomedCLIP ViT-B] · [BrainIAC] · [BrainLM] · frontier/open figures estimated
Same models as the "hungry" slide, re-asked along a new axis. Beat 1: the model names fly from the left list onto their own bubbles. Beat 2 (single move): the new y-axis — MODEL PARAMETERS — flashes in as the bubbles rescale, and the gap explodes — neuroimaging FMs sit at tens of millions of parameters (BrainGFM ~25M, BrainIAC ~46M, MedSAM ~94M, BrainLM ~111M, BiomedCLIP ~200M) while frontier LLMs tower 3-5 orders of magnitude higher (GPT-3 175B, Gemma 4 ~27B, Qwen3.6 ~235B, frontier closed ~1T+). That is the federation challenge: we must federate models this large over hospital networks that will never centralize the data. Sparse Federated Models

  • Huge models → impossible bandwidth
  • Federate only a sparse subnetwork
  • 50% sparse: matches dense
  • 90% sparse: 10× less comms, <10% drop
  • Foundation-scale FL becomes feasible
Gender classification, ABCD MRI (21 sites)
Method: [Ohib, Thapaliya, Dziugaite, Liu, Calhoun & Plis — SSFL, TMLR 2025]
This is the payoff to the previous slide: models are huge and hospitals won't centralize, so the bottleneck is communication. Sparse federated learning answers it directly — we federate only a sparse subnetwork, exchanging a few percent of the weights. Up to 50% sparsity matches or beats the dense baselines; at 90% sparsity we cut communication 10x with under a 10% accuracy drop. The "Ours" curve stays flat while every other sparse method collapses. That's what makes federating foundation-scale models realistic on hospital networks — the future direction this talk is pointing toward. (Result shown on ABCD gender classification across 21 sites; method = SSFL, TMLR 2025.)

Federated Foundation Encoders

A single node may hold PRIVATE data in any number of modalities. Pre-trained uni-modal FOUNDATION encoders map each modality into a unified EMBEDDING space; on top sits a task-specific SPARSE model where each modality is represented by only a small subset of parameters, trained by backprop against the node's PRIVATE labels. The federation mechanics (sparse modality-specific gradient exchange with an aggregator) are deliberately left out here -- this slide is just the per-node picture.

Inside-Out Federated Learning

Future-methods slide (Category 1) aimed at making Cat 3 platforms future-proof. Standard FL assumes the whole model updates everywhere. Foundation models flip that: most of the model frozen and shared, only small parts federated (low-dim adapters / sparse subsets). Align "inside" (big model) and "outside" (sites) to decentralized network constraints.
